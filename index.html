SHADES OF BLUE E-LEARNING WEBCAST The SHADES OF BLUE E-Learning Webcast is an interactive online presentation that is in harmony with the SHADES OF BLUE STEM educational initiatives that engage and educate students. This exciting "Question & Answer" session streamed live via the internet to our goal of over 10 million children nationwide will expose students and instructors alike to Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics (STEM) 'subject matter experts' including Tuskegee Airmen, astronauts, public policy makers, scientists and other professionals. http://www.ourshadesofblue.org/ -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Dates & Times To accommodate both the East & West coasts, two webcasts per day have been scheduled. Broadcast from: NASA Goddard Space Flight Center Digital Learning Network Studio For more information on the NASA Digital Learning Network Visit Their Website SHADES OF BLUE E-LEARNING WEBCAST REGISTRATION FORM Sign Up Today! Deadline: September 30, 2016 SHADES OF BLUE is a non-profit educational organization dedicated to mentoring, tutoring, counseling, and arranging referrals for young people who desire to pursue STEM careers. Although SHADES OF BLUE primarily focuses on aviation and aerospace, the same level of assistance is available to all students. Within the organization are numerous professionals subject matter experts' who provide a support system for mentoring and job placement that will maximize the probability of success of students who choose a career in STEM related fields. SHADES OF BLUE has developed a system to identify and monitor students from elementary school through college. In addition, SHADES OF BLUE has developed partnerships and strong relationships with many businesses and educational groups within the STEM oriented fields, who are able to provide graduates with potential future employment and/or educational opportunities. They believe as we do, that as leaders, we need to help and assist in setting early educational paths for our youth that adhere to our dedicated focus of encouraging kids to enter the aerospace, aviation and scientific career fields with a passion that deserves our very best effort. Over 500 million light years from our home the Cartwheel Galaxy glows bright with a ring of star formation,...

September 02, 2016 09:52 am

Over 500 million light years from our home the Cartwheel Galaxy glows bright with a ring of star formation, evidence of a collision with another galaxy. The school of thought about this image is that about 100 million years ago a smaller galaxy collided head on with the galaxy, creating a massive shockwave that quickly swept gas and dust in its path. This concentration of gas and dust on the front of the shockwave created the perfect conditions for star formation, sparking the coalescence of millions of young and hot stars. The hottest, and shortest lived, of these stars glow bright blue, allowing astronomers to trace the shockwave’s motion through the galaxy. These stars shine brightest in the ultraviolet wavelengths of light, which are shown in the image as blue and are from NASA’s GALEX mission. This false color image also contains a composite of images from NASA’s Chandra X-Ray Observatory, which appear as purple and detail the highest energy areas, NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope, which are shown as green and capture the light from stars similar to our Sun in the visible wavelengths of light as well as glowing gas, and NASA’s Spitzer Space Telescope, which are colored red and capture the warm dust clouds and cooler stars. The combination of all of these wavelengths from four different NASA space observatories into one image provides a glimpse into the galaxy’s fascinating and violent past. Image Credit: NASA/JPL-Caltech

Save the Date: On August 21, 2017, we will be treated to the first total eclipse of the Sun visible in...

August 26, 2016 04:25 pm

Save the Date: On August 21, 2017, we will be treated to the first total eclipse of the Sun visible in the continental U.S. in almost 40 years. The spectacular total eclipse will only be visible in a narrow band about 60 miles across, stretching diagonally across the country from a beach in Oregon to a beach in South Carolina. Everyone in North America located outside the narrow band will see a partial solar eclipse, where a big "bite" will be taken out of the Sun.
